# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Freelancers and small agency teams. Two modes of use, one core behavior:

- **Solo freelancers** at their desk, mid-project, needing to log time against a client or task without losing focus. Context is scattered — they switch between a code editor, a client email, and this tool in the same minute.
- **Agency leads and managers** overseeing a small team (2–15 people), tracking who is working on what across multiple active projects. They check the dashboard periodically, not obsessively.

Both users share the same constraint: Bee Time is not where they spend their day. It is the tool they reach for, use in 30 seconds, and leave.

## Product Purpose

Bee Time is a time tracking application for freelancers and small agencies. It handles projects, clients, members, and timesheets — organized around organizations so it scales from a solo practitioner to a small team without mode-switching.

Success looks like: user opens the app, logs hours on a task or project, and is back to their real work in under a minute. The longer a user lingers in the UI, the more the product has failed.

## Brand Personality

Quiet confidence. Calm and unhurried. The visual language of a well-organized physical notebook: structured, warm, nothing to prove.

Three words: **focused, warm, unhurried**.

Emotional goal: users should feel in control, not managed. The app accommodates their workflow; it does not impose one.

Reference products that capture the right register:

- **Notion** — warm neutral palette, flexible structure, feels like personal space rather than corporate software.
- **Toggl** — friendly and low-friction, doesn't take itself too seriously, gets out of the way fast.

## Anti-references

- **Enterprise SaaS (Jira, Asana, Harvest enterprise)** — dense, form-heavy, every action nested in dropdowns. Over-organized to the point of obstruction.
- **Legacy invoicing tools** — table-heavy, no visual hierarchy, feels like 2009 accounting software.
- **Bootstrap admin templates** — blue headers, gray data tables, identical column widths, no identity.
- **Gradient-heavy startup SaaS** — hero metrics, big glowing numbers, decorative color everywhere. Noise pretending to be signal.

## Design Principles

1. **Get out of the way.** Every interaction should feel faster than expected. The app is a means, not a destination. If a task takes two taps where one would do, it needs to be rethought.

2. **Warm precision.** The golden palette and warm neutrals signal approachability without sacrificing clarity. Warmth is not looseness — it is comfort in a well-designed tool. Never decorate where structure is sufficient.

3. **Trust the user.** Don't over-explain with tooltips, empty-state essays, or onboarding walls. Show the interface; let it be learned. Expert users reward this. Beginners adapt faster than popups suggest they do.

4. **Restraint as craft.** The absence of visual noise is a design choice, not laziness. Blank space, tight type scales, and a minimal accent color are commitments, not defaults. Push back on every new visual element: does this earn its place?

5. **Consistent at any scale.** The experience for a solo freelancer with 3 clients should feel identical to the experience for an agency team with 30 projects. The product must not add complexity when given more data.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target: WCAG 2.1 AA across all surfaces. Color contrast ratios for text and interactive elements must meet or exceed 4.5:1 (normal text) and 3:1 (large text and UI components). Keyboard navigation must be complete for all data entry and primary actions. Reduced-motion preference must suppress all non-essential animations.
